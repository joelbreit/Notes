# Vocab Flashcards

## 2025

**depravity**: moral corruption; wickedness
**Baumol’s Cost Disease**: the tendency for wages in jobs that have experienced little or no increase in labor productivity to rise in response to rising wages in other jobs that did experience high productivity growth
**schlep (Yiddish)**: haul or carry with great effort
**cf. (confer)**: compare with; consult
**bulwark**: the extension of a wall above the base level
**bastion**: a protruding  part of a fortification built to allow line of sight on the primary portion
**nonce**: an arbitrary number that can be used just once in a cryptographic communication
**occasionalism**: a word created for a single occasion or utterance but not otherwise recognized as a word in a language
**coalesce**: come together to form one mass or whole
**Moravec’s Paradox**: the inverse relationship in AI between simple tasks for humans and simple tasks for computers
**Berkson's Paradox**: phenomena where selecting criteria for a subpopulation can create an inverted relationship between variables in that group relative to the larger population
**Parkinson’s Law**: work expands to fill the time available for its completion
**compersion**: the feeling of joy one has experiencing another's joy
**Laplace’s Rule of Succession**: estimating the probability of a binary event based as `(num occurrences + 1) / (num trials + 2)`

## 2024

**extrapolate**: extend an existing trend to an unknown situation by assuming that similar methods will be applicable
**Jevons Paradox**: phenomena where increases in the efficiency of producing X tends to increase (rather than decrease) the rate of consumption of X
**eponymous**: being the person whom something is named after or a thing named after a particular person
**eponym**: a person after whom something is named a thing named after a particular person
**desiderata**: something that is needed or wanted
**equanimity**: mental calmness, composure, and evenness of temper, especially in a difficult situation
**sanguine**: optimistic or positive, especially in an apparently bad or difficult situation
**redux**: brought back; revived
**posthaste**: with great speed or urgency
**chic**: elegantly and stylishly fashionable
**gauche**: lacking ease or grace; unsophisticated and socially awkward
**kitsch**: art, objects, or design considered to be in poor taste because of excessive garishness or sentimentality, but sometimes appreciated in an ironic or knowing way
**garish**: obtrusively bright and showy; lurid
**lurid**: very vivid in color, especially so as to create an unpleasantly harsh or unnatural effect
**obtrusive**: noticeable or prominent in an unwelcome or intrusive way
**stodgy**: dull and uninspired; old-fashioned
**purlieus**: the area near or surrounding a place
**nominative determinism**: the hypothesis that people tend to gravitate towards areas of work that fit their names
**maturation**: the process of becoming mature or fully developed
**vis-à-vis (French)**: (literally "face to face") in relation to; compared with
**Laffer Curve**: theoretical relationship between taxes and the economy suggesting an optimal tax rate for maximizing revenue
**diaspora**: the dispersion of any people from their original homeland or the community formed by such a dispersion
**troglodyte**: a person who lives in a cave (and is ignorant and old-fashioned)
**veracity**: conformity to facts; accuracy
**Chesterton’s Fence**: the principle that reforms should not be made until the reasoning behind the status quo is understood
**Occam’s Razor**: all else equal, the simplest explanation is usually the correct one
**transitory**: not permanent
**amortize**: gradually write off the initial cost of (an asset) over a period
**Law of Comparative Advantage**: the economic theory that entities should specialize in producing that which they can produce more efficiently than other goods, even if they can produce those other goods more efficiently than other entities can
**Efficient Market Hypothesis**: theory that prices fully reflect available information, making it impossible to consistently achieve higher returns than the market
**Coase Theorem**: the economic theory that under certain conditions, private parties can negotiate without cost over the allocation of resources and solve the problem of externalities on their own
**de novo (latin)**: (literally "of new") starting from the beginning; anew
**Osborne Effect**: the phenomenon where announcing a future product prematurely causes customers to stop buying the current product
